# The Dataset

## What problem are we solving?

When a brand designs a new lipstick, someone has to decide *how it will be packaged*: the
tube, the cap, the carton it ships in, the shrink wrap, and so on. Each of those parts is
made of some material (plastic, paperboard, metal…) and weighs a certain amount. Together,
that list is called a **Bill of Materials**, or **BOM** - basically a packaging recipe.

We want a model that can look at a product and predict its packaging recipe automatically.
To teach it, we need lots of examples of products *together with* their correct packaging
recipe. That collection of examples is the dataset.

## Where does the data come from?

The data is **synthetic** - it was generated by a separate tool (in a sibling project called
`mecca-streamlit`), not collected by hand from real shelves. The generation run produced
roughly **49,881 lipstick products**, and each one comes with a realistic, fully detailed
packaging breakdown.

We use synthetic data because:

- **It's complete.** Every product already has a correct packaging recipe attached. Real-world
  data is usually missing this, and labelling thousands of products by hand would be slow and
  expensive.
- **It's consistent.** Every record follows the same structure, so the model sees clean,
  uniform examples.
- **It's free and repeatable.** We can regenerate or re-sample it any time.

## What does one example look like?

Think of each example as a product card with two halves:

1. **The product** - its name, brand, size, where it's made and sold, and a short marketing
   description. For instance: *"Velour Lip Colour" by Botanica Beauty, 3.5 g, made in China,
   sold in Australia - "a creamy matte lipstick in a natural botanical shade."*

2. **The packaging recipe** - the list of every packaging part, grouped into three tiers:
   - **Primary** - what directly holds the product (the lipstick tube/barrel and its cap).
   - **Secondary** - what the primary sits inside (a unit carton or box).
   - **Tertiary** - bulk/shipping packaging (the outer shipping case, dividers, wrap).

   For each part we know its size, what materials it's made of, and how much each weighs.

## How much of it do we actually use?

The full set is large, so we take a **representative random sample of 10,000 products** to
work with. The sampling is deliberately *reproducible* - running it again always picks the
same 10,000 products - so results can be compared run to run.

Every product also gets a stable ID (a "SKU") so we can always trace an example back to its
source and keep the same product from accidentally landing in two different parts of the
project.

## One thing to keep in mind

In this particular run, **every product is a lipstick.** The system is built to generalise to
other categories later, but the current dataset is focused on one product type. That focus is
intentional - it keeps this first version simple and accurate within a narrow domain.
